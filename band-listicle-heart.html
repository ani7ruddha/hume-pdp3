The problem is not that medicine is failing us. The problem is that traditional medicine is fundamentally reactive. The 5 Silent Signs Your Heart Is In Trouble

The following five signs are not dramatic. They don't send you to the ER. They are subtle shifts in physiological data that your body is generating around the clock — and that the right technology can now detect and trend for you continuously. Each one, on its own, is worth monitoring. All five together can give you the kind of cardiac risk picture that used to require a cardiologist's office.

Silent Sign #1
Your HRV Is Declining and You Don't Know It

Ask most people what their HRV is and you'll get a blank stare. Heart Rate Variability is the variation in time between successive heartbeats — and despite its technical-sounding name, it is one of the most powerful biomarkers in all of cardiovascular medicine. A high HRV indicates a heart that is adaptable, responsive, and well-regulated by a healthy autonomic nervous system. A low or declining HRV tells a very different story.

Research published in cardiology literature consistently shows that declining HRV is associated with increased risk of ventricular arrhythmias, sudden cardiac death, and overall cardiovascular mortality. Studies following cardiac patients over time have found that HRV metrics begin deteriorating 6 to 12 months — and in some cases longer — before a major cardiac event. This is not a minor association. It is one of the clearest biological early warning signals that medicine has ever identified for cardiovascular disease.

The problem is that HRV has historically been measured only in clinical settings, on an ECG, at a single point in time. A snapshot HRV tells you almost nothing. What matters is the trajectory: is your HRV this week lower than it was last month? Has it been quietly declining for the past six months while you've been attributing your fatigue to "just getting older"?

This is where continuous wearable measurement changes everything. By capturing your HRV nightly and trending it over weeks and months, you can see the kind of gradual decline that no annual physical will ever reveal — and intervene before it becomes a crisis.

Silent Sign #2
Your Blood Oxygen Dips While You Sleep

You're sleeping soundly — or so you think. You have no idea that dozens of times each night, your blood oxygen saturation (SpO2) is dropping from a healthy 97–99% down to 90%, 87%, maybe lower. You wake up each morning tired, a little foggy, and wonder why a full night of sleep doesn't seem to restore you. You assume it's stress, or age, or just "how things are now."

What you may actually be experiencing is nocturnal hypoxia — repeated drops in blood oxygen during sleep that are characteristic of sleep-disordered breathing conditions like obstructive sleep apnea. And the cardiac consequences of untreated sleep apnea are severe and well-documented. People with moderate-to-severe obstructive sleep apnea have a dramatically elevated risk of hypertension, atrial fibrillation, coronary artery disease, and stroke compared to those without the condition. The repeated oxygen deprivation and arousal events place enormous stress on the cardiovascular system night after night.

The challenge is that most people with sleep apnea have no idea they have it. The classic symptom — loud snoring — is often reported by a partner rather than recognized by the patient. Daytime fatigue is attributed to a busy schedule. Without monitoring SpO2 throughout the night, these events are entirely invisible. A standard sleep study can diagnose the condition, but it requires scheduling, an overnight clinic visit, and typically significant cost. Continuous nightly SpO2 monitoring at home can alert you to patterns worth investigating — potentially months or years before a cardiac consequence emerges.

Silent Sign #3
Your Resting Heart Rate Is Slowly Rising

This one is particularly insidious because the change is so gradual. Your resting heart rate at age 40 was 62 BPM. By 46 it crept to 68. Now you're 52 and it's sitting at 74, and you've never noticed because no single day looked dramatically different from the day before. But that 12-beat-per-minute increase is not trivial. Not even close.

Large-scale epidemiological studies involving hundreds of thousands of participants have found that every 10-beat-per-minute increase in resting heart rate is associated with an approximately 18% increase in cardiovascular mortality risk, independent of other risk factors. A resting heart rate above 80 BPM has been associated with a two-fold increase in risk compared to a resting heart rate below 60 BPM in several prospective cohort studies. Elevated resting heart rate reflects reduced cardiac efficiency, increased sympathetic nervous system tone, and a heart that is working harder than it should be just to maintain baseline function.

The critical word here is trend. A single resting heart rate reading — the kind you get at a doctor's visit, often while slightly anxious and having just rushed across a parking lot — is almost meaningless in isolation. What matters is whether your true resting heart rate, measured consistently under standardized conditions, is stable or drifting upward over months and years. This requires the kind of continuous, daily measurement that has simply not been possible outside of expensive clinical monitoring until recently.

Silent Sign #4
You Never Fully Recover From Physical Stress

You used to go for a long run on Saturday and feel fine by Sunday morning. Now it takes until Wednesday before your energy fully returns. You assume this is simply aging — your recovery capacity declining as your body gets older. And while some decrease in recovery speed is expected with age, a persistent, progressive inability to fully recover from physical stress is a meaningful signal about your autonomic nervous system — and your heart.

Recovery, as measured by HRV and resting heart rate trends after physical exertion, reflects the balance between your sympathetic nervous system ("fight or flight") and parasympathetic nervous system ("rest and digest"). A heart and autonomic system in good shape can shift efficiently from sympathetic activation during exercise back to parasympathetic dominance during rest. When this shift becomes sluggish — when you spend more time in sympathetic overdrive — it places continuous low-grade stress on the cardiovascular system. Chronically elevated sympathetic tone is associated with hypertension, arrhythmia risk, and accelerated progression of coronary artery disease.

Poor recovery is also closely linked to chronic inflammation — another major driver of atherosclerosis and cardiac risk. When the body cannot adequately repair the microscopic muscle and tissue damage from physical stress, it remains in a mild but persistent inflammatory state. Over months and years, this chronic inflammation contributes to arterial wall damage and plaque formation. The inability to recover fully is not a cosmetic problem. It is your body telling you that your autonomic nervous system is overwhelmed.

Silent Sign #5
Your Sleep Is Getting Fragmented

You're spending eight hours in bed but waking up exhausted. You might not remember waking during the night — but your body does. Sleep fragmentation, even in the absence of conscious awakening, disrupts the restorative architecture of deep sleep and REM cycles, and the cardiovascular consequences of chronically fragmented sleep are now among the most well-established links in sleep medicine.

During deep, uninterrupted sleep, blood pressure drops, heart rate slows, and the cardiovascular system undergoes essential repair processes. This nocturnal dipping of blood pressure is considered a protective phenomenon. People who fail to experience adequate nocturnal blood pressure dipping — often due to fragmented sleep — have significantly higher rates of cardiovascular events compared to those who do. Studies published in major cardiology and sleep medicine journals have found that fragmented sleep is associated with elevated inflammatory markers including C-reactive protein and interleukin-6, both of which accelerate arterial damage and atherosclerosis.

Poor sleep also elevates cortisol levels chronically. Cortisol, the primary stress hormone, constricts arteries, raises blood pressure, promotes the deposition of visceral fat, and impairs insulin sensitivity — all of which independently increase cardiovascular risk. A single night of bad sleep is inconsequential. But months and years of quietly fragmented sleep, undetected and untreated, amount to a sustained cardiovascular assault that never shows up on your annual cholesterol panel.

What Your HRV Number Actually Means — And Why Most People Are Flying Blind

HRV is measured in milliseconds — the variation between individual heartbeats. A heart beating at 60 BPM is not beating once every exact second. It beats at, say, 980ms, then 1,040ms, then 995ms, then 1,060ms. That variation — driven by the constant push-pull between your sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems — is your HRV. More variation is better. Less variation means your autonomic nervous system has less flexibility and adaptability.

HRV norms vary considerably by age, sex, and fitness level. A 60-year-old's "healthy" HRV will look different from a 35-year-old's. What matters far more than your absolute number is your personal trend. Is your HRV stable? Is it improving with better sleep, less alcohol, and more consistent exercise? Or is it quietly declining despite no obvious changes in your lifestyle — which may be your body's earliest detectable signal that something is wrong systemically?

Key Insight: Researchers at major academic medical centers have found that HRV begins to decline measurably in the months to years before major cardiac events in patients who subsequently suffered heart attacks — even when standard clinical markers like cholesterol and blood pressure appeared acceptable. HRV is a leading indicator, not a lagging one. Traditional diagnostics are largely lagging indicators. That's the gap Hume was designed to fill.
