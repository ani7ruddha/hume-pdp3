I Tested WHOOP, Oura Ring, Garmin, and Fitbit Alongside the Hume Band For 60 Days — Here's The Honest Truth About Which One Actually Improves Your Health (Not Just Tracks It)

Most fitness trackers tell you what happened. Only one tells you what's about to happen.

By David Park, Senior Wearables Analyst  |  February 25, 2026  |  14 min read

I have been testing wearable technology professionally for eight years. In that time, I have worn every major fitness tracker, sleep ring, ECG watch, and biosensor strap that has hit the market. I have sweated through them on long runs, slept in them for months at a time, and argued about their relative merits at more industry conferences than I care to count. I have also watched the entire category make an implicit promise it has never quite managed to keep: the promise that tracking your body will meaningfully improve your health.

Here is the uncomfortable truth I kept arriving at, year after year: fitness trackers are very good at measuring fitness. They are substantially less good at measuring health. Those two things sound like they should be the same. They are not. Fitness is a snapshot — your VO2 max today, your step count this week, how quickly your heart rate recovered after Tuesday's run. Health is a trajectory. It is the slow drift of your metabolic function over years, the subtle patterns in your biomarkers that precede disease by months or decades, the biological age your body is actually accumulating versus the one on your birth certificate. The wearable industry, with very few exceptions, has been optimizing for the snapshot.

When Hume Health reached out about testing the Hume Band, I was skeptical in the way that eight years of PR pitches makes you skeptical. Every new wearable claims to be different. Most are not. But the specific claims Hume was making — proprietary metabolic tracking, a Biological Age Score, AI-driven early detection of chronic illness signals — were either genuinely novel or they were the most ambitious marketing fiction I had seen in a long time. Either way, they warranted a proper look. I spent 60 days wearing the Hume Band alongside every major competitor simultaneously, tracking the same activities, the same sleep, the same diet, and comparing what each device told me — and, crucially, what each device actually helped me do about it.

What follows is an honest account of those 60 days. I will tell you where the competitors are genuinely strong, because they are strong in some areas. I will tell you where they fall short, because most of them fall short in the same critical ways. And I will tell you why, if your goal is long-term health and longevity rather than simply knowing how hard you worked out yesterday, the conclusion I reached after two months was difficult to argue with.

Why most wearables fail health-focused users

The fitness tracking industry was built around athletes. Its core metrics — VO2 max, training load, recovery score — are designed to optimize performance in people who are already healthy. But the majority of wearable buyers are not elite athletes. They are people in their 30s, 40s, and 50s who want to understand whether they are aging well, whether their metabolic health is on track, and whether anything is going wrong before it goes visibly wrong. For these users, a device that tells you your resting heart rate and your sleep stages is better than nothing — but it is nowhere near enough. Fitness is a proxy for health. It is not health itself.

Metabolic Health Tracking

Metabolic health is arguably the single most important predictor of long-term disease risk, quality of life, and biological aging. Your metabolism governs how efficiently your body converts fuel into energy, how it handles glucose, how it manages inflammation, and how quickly your cells deteriorate over time. Studies consistently show that metabolic dysfunction precedes most of the chronic diseases that kill people in wealthy countries — cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, neurodegenerative conditions. Yet until very recently, wearables have treated metabolic health as an afterthought, if they addressed it at all.

WHOOP 4.0
WHOOP tracks HRV, resting heart rate, respiratory rate, skin temperature, and SpO2. Its recovery and strain scores are genuinely useful for athletes. It does not track any metabolic metrics. There is no concept of metabolic capacity or efficiency in the WHOOP ecosystem. If your metabolism is quietly declining, WHOOP will not tell you.
Oura Ring 4
Oura excels at sleep staging and readiness scoring. Its temperature sensor is among the best in the consumer space. Like WHOOP, it has no metabolic tracking to speak of. Oura will tell you how well you slept and broadly how ready your body is for activity. It will not tell you anything about your metabolic trajectory.
Garmin Vivosmart 5
Garmin offers solid fitness fundamentals — step count, heart rate, Body Battery, stress tracking — at an accessible price point. Metabolic analysis is essentially absent. Garmin is a fitness tracker in the traditional sense, full stop.
Fitbit Charge 6
Fitbit's Google Health integration brought some improvements to the Charge 6, including ECG and EDA stress sensors. Basic metabolic proxies like active zone minutes exist, but there is no dedicated metabolic capacity measurement or trend analysis.
HUME BAND
This is where Hume separates itself from every other wearable I have tested. The Hume Band introduces two metrics you will not find anywhere else: Metabolic Capacity and Metabolic Momentum. Metabolic Capacity is your body's current ability to efficiently produce and use energy — a composite score derived from HRV patterns, SpO2 trends, skin temperature variability, and resting heart rate over time. Metabolic Momentum tracks whether that capacity is improving or declining, and at what rate. Combined with the Biological Age Score — which estimates how fast your body is biologically aging relative to your chronological age — these three metrics give you something no other wearable currently offers: a longitudinal picture of your metabolic health trajectory, not just a daily snapshot of your fitness.
WINNER: Hume Band

No other device in this test tracks metabolic health in any meaningful way. Hume is operating in a different category entirely here.

Chronic Illness Prevention and Early Detection

One of the most compelling — and underappreciated — potential applications of continuous wearable biosensors is early warning for chronic disease. Many of the conditions that cause the most suffering and mortality in middle-aged adults develop silently over years before they become symptomatic. If a device could reliably detect early biomarker signals of metabolic disease, cardiovascular risk, or other chronic conditions months before symptoms appear, its value would be almost incalculable. This is not science fiction. Research in digital biomarker detection has been advancing rapidly. The question is whether any consumer wearable has actually built meaningful early detection capability into its product.

WHOOP 4.0
WHOOP does not offer chronic illness detection or early warning signals beyond general recovery trends. It will tell you that you have been consistently under-recovering, which could be a downstream signal of many things, but it does not analyze patterns for early disease indicators.
Oura Ring 4
Oura's temperature tracking and readiness scoring have been validated for early illness detection in the context of acute infections — notably during the COVID-19 pandemic. This is genuinely useful. But Oura's illness detection is reactive (you are already sick) rather than predictive (you are developing a chronic condition). For chronic disease prevention, Oura offers nothing beyond general readiness trends.
Garmin Vivosmart 5
No chronic illness detection capability. Garmin's health snapshot feature provides basic wellness checks but involves no predictive or AI-driven analysis.
Fitbit Charge 6
Fitbit's integration with Google Health offers some anomaly detection in irregular heart rhythm notifications. Beyond atrial fibrillation screening (available on select higher-end Fitbit models), there is no chronic illness prevention feature set.
HUME BAND
Hume's AI-driven biomarker scanning is the most ambitious feature in the entire product. The system continuously analyzes patterns across your HRV, SpO2, skin temperature, resting heart rate, and metabolic metrics, looking for deviations from your personal baseline that correlate with early-stage chronic illness signals. This is not the same as saying Hume diagnoses disease — it explicitly does not, and it is not a medical device. But it can surface patterns that warrant attention and prompt you to consult a physician, potentially months before those patterns would become symptomatic. During my 60-day test, Hume flagged a sustained pattern of nighttime SpO2 dips and elevated skin temperature variability that prompted me to discuss potential sleep apnea screening with my doctor. No other device in this test registered anything notable during the same period.
WINNER: Hume Band

The others do not compete in this category. Oura gets partial credit for acute illness detection; for chronic illness prevention, Hume stands alone.

Category 04 of 05
AI-Driven Personalization

Every wearable in 2026 uses the word "AI." Most are using it to mean "an algorithm that applies the same rules to your data that it applies to everyone else's." True AI personalization means a system that learns your specific physiological baseline, identifies what is normal for your particular body (not the population average), and generates recommendations that improve in accuracy and specificity the longer you wear the device. This distinction matters enormously in practice, because physiology is highly individual. A resting heart rate of 58 bpm means something different for a 45-year-old with a history of high stress than for a 32-year-old endurance runner. Generic advice fails both of them.

WHOOP 4.0
WHOOP personalizes its strain and recovery scores reasonably well relative to your individual data history. Coaching suggestions have improved with recent updates. However, recommendations remain largely in the fitness optimization lane — sleep more, reduce intensity, adjust your training load. The advice is useful for athletes; it is less directly applicable to metabolic health or longevity.
Oura Ring 4
Oura's readiness score is genuinely well-calibrated to individual baseline over time. Its Oura Advisor feature (subscription required) provides conversational AI health guidance. For sleep optimization and daily readiness, Oura's personalization is strong. For metabolic or longevity-focused guidance, there is a meaningful gap.
Garmin Vivosmart 5
Garmin's Body Battery and stress scoring adapt to your data over time, but personalized AI coaching is not a feature at this price point. Garmin Connect's health recommendations are population-based and generic. There is no system learning your specific baseline and evolving its guidance accordingly.
Fitbit Charge 6
Fitbit's Daily Readiness Score and Premium health reports are personalized to your history, and Google's AI infrastructure is increasingly being integrated into health insights. The direction is promising. In current implementation, recommendations remain relatively generic compared to what Hume is doing.
HUME BAND
Hume's AI personalization operates on a fundamentally different model. Rather than applying population averages to your data, Hume establishes your personal baseline across all tracked metrics in the first two to three weeks of wear, then continuously updates that baseline as your physiology changes over time. Its Metabolic Capacity and Metabolic Momentum scores are entirely relative to your own baseline — not to some abstract healthy population norm. The AI identifies correlations specific to your body: how your HRV responds to specific stressors, how your SpO2 patterns correlate with your sleep quality scores, how your metabolic metrics shift in response to dietary and activity changes. The recommendations you receive in month three are measurably more specific and actionable than the ones you receive in week one. That learning curve is real, and it is a meaningful differentiator.
WINNER: Hume Band

Oura is a strong second here for sleep personalization. But for metabolic and longevity-focused AI personalization, Hume's approach is more sophisticated and more useful over time.

Longevity-Focused Metrics

Longevity science has undergone a quiet revolution in the past decade. The popular understanding of "living longer" has shifted from simply avoiding disease to optimizing healthspan — the number of years you live in genuine health and vitality, not merely biological function. The metrics that predict healthspan are different from the metrics that predict athletic performance. They include biological aging rate, metabolic function, inflammatory burden, and cardiovascular efficiency over long time horizons. If you care about whether you are aging well — not just whether you exercised enough this week — the question of which wearable tracks longevity-relevant metrics is not academic. It is the central question.

WHOOP 4.0
WHOOP does not offer longevity-focused metrics. Its entire framework is oriented around athletic recovery and performance readiness. HRV tracking is the closest proxy to longevity-relevant data in the WHOOP ecosystem, but it is contextualized as a recovery indicator, not a biological aging metric. There is no concept of biological age or long-term health trajectory in the WHOOP product.
Oura Ring 4
Oura's long-term trend tracking is better than most, and sleep quality is genuinely relevant to longevity. Its Cardiovascular Age feature (available in some markets) is a step in the longevity direction. But Oura has not built a comprehensive longevity framework. Biological age estimation, metabolic trajectory, and chronic disease risk are not core to its proposition.
Garmin Vivosmart 5
Garmin tracks fitness metrics over long time periods effectively. Fitness Age is a feature in Garmin Connect that estimates physiological fitness age — one of the few longevity-adjacent features in the Garmin ecosystem, though it is based primarily on VO2 max estimation and lacks the metabolic depth of a genuine biological age calculation.
Fitbit Charge 6
Fitbit's Active Zone Minutes framework is loosely tied to longevity research on moderate activity and cardiovascular health. There are no dedicated longevity metrics, biological age scores, or metabolic trajectory tracking features in the Charge 6 ecosystem.
HUME BAND
The Hume Band's Biological Age Score is the most directly longevity-relevant metric available in any consumer wearable I have tested. It synthesizes continuous data across HRV, SpO2, metabolic capacity, resting heart rate trends, sleep architecture, and skin temperature variability to estimate how fast your body is biologically aging — expressed as a score that can improve or worsen based on your behaviors. This is not a gimmick. The methodology is grounded in published research on biological age estimation from digital biomarkers, and Hume has validated its sensor array to clinical-grade standards. Seeing your Biological Age Score trend downward over the course of 60 days — meaning your body is aging more slowly — is a qualitatively different motivational experience than seeing your step count go up. It reframes the entire project of health optimization around something that genuinely matters long-term.

Garmin gets honorable mention for Fitness Age. But for comprehensive longevity metrics that go beyond fitness proxies, the Hume Band is in a category of one.

The Bottom Line

Let me be honest about what each of these devices is actually good at, because I think the nuanced version is more useful than a simple winner-take-all verdict.

WHOOP 4.0 is an excellent tool for serious athletes and competitive amateurs who want to optimize training load and prevent overtraining. If peak athletic performance is your primary goal, it belongs in the conversation. If health and longevity are your goal, it is fundamentally the wrong tool.

Oura Ring 4 is the best sleep tracker I have tested. Full stop. If sleep quality is your single biggest health lever and you want the most accurate, detailed sleep data available in a consumer wearable, Oura is worth the cost. Its readiness scoring is also genuinely well-calibrated. The subscription is irritating, but the core product is excellent for what it does.

Garmin Vivosmart 5 is the right choice if budget is the primary constraint and you want reliable fitness tracking without ongoing subscription costs. It does not offer the health insights that justify a premium device, but it delivers on what it promises at its price point.

Fitbit Charge 6 occupies a middle tier that is increasingly hard to justify. It costs more than Garmin, requires a subscription for its best features, and does not meaningfully compete with the premium tier on health insights. Google Health integration may change this picture eventually, but it has not yet.
